Harriet Tubman

Harriet Tubman (born around 1822, died in 1913) was an American abolitionist, humanitarian, and activist who played a critical role in helping enslaved African Americans escape to freedom through the Underground Railroad. Born into slavery in Maryland, Tubman escaped in 1849 and then made over a dozen dangerous trips back to the South to lead other enslaved people to freedom.

How Did Harriet Tubman Change the World?

Harriet Tubman, a former enslaved woman, became a key figure in the Underground Railroad, guiding over 300 enslaved people to freedom while also serving as a spy and nurse for the Union Army during the Civil War, and later advocating for women’s suffrage and racial equality.

Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.

Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. (born January 15, 1929 – died April 4, 1968) was a Baptist minister, civil rights leader, and activist who became one of the most prominent figures in the struggle for racial equality in the United States. His leadership in the American civil rights movement of the 1950s and 1960s led to significant changes in laws, policies, and public attitudes about racial justice and human rights.

How Did Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. Change the World?

Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. led the Civil Rights Movement with a philosophy of nonviolent protest, spearheading iconic events like the Montgomery Bus Boycott and the March on Washington, advocating for racial equality, economic justice, and civil rights legislation, and inspiring generations with his vision of a “Beloved Community”.

Malcolm X

Malcolm X (born Malcolm Little on May 19, 1925 – died February 21, 1965) was a civil rights activist, minister, and spokesperson for the Nation of Islam (NOI) who became one of the most influential and controversial figures in the fight for Black empowerment and racial justice. Unlike many leaders of the time, Malcolm X advocated for Black pride, self-defense, and self-determination, challenging the more mainstream philosophy of nonviolence promoted by leaders like Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.

How Did Malcolm X Change the World?

Malcolm X, initially a member of the Nation of Islam, challenged racial injustice through powerful speeches advocating for Black self-determination, self-defense, and Black pride, later embracing broader human rights causes and influencing the Black Power Movement after his pilgrimage to Mecca.

Rosa Parks

Rosa Parks (born February 4, 1913 – died October 24, 2005) was an African American civil rights activist, often called “The Mother of the Civil Rights Movement.” Her refusal to give up her seat to a white passenger on a segregated bus in Montgomery, Alabama, in 1955 sparked the Montgomery Bus Boycott, a key event that helped launch the modern civil rights movement in the United States.

How Did Rosa Parks Change the World?

Rosa Parks’ refusal to give up her seat on a segregated bus in Montgomery, Alabama, in 1955 sparked the Montgomery Bus Boycott, helping to catalyze the Civil Rights Movement, and later, she remained an active advocate for racial justice and women’s rights.

Nelson Mandela

Nelson Mandela (born July 18, 1918 – died December 5, 2013) was a South African anti-apartheid revolutionary, political leader, and philanthropist who served as the first Black president of South Africa from 1994 to 1999. Mandela is best known for his decades-long fight against apartheid, a brutal system of racial segregation and white supremacy enforced by South Africa’s government. His imprisonment for 27 years and subsequent leadership in creating a peaceful transition to democracy made him a global symbol of justice, reconciliation, and forgiveness.

How Did Nelson Mandela Change the World?

Nelson Mandela spent 27 years in prison for fighting apartheid, later becoming South Africa’s first Black president, leading the peaceful transition to democracy, and advocating for reconciliation, human rights, and global social justice, leaving a legacy of forgiveness and unity.

Frederick Douglass

Frederick Douglass (born February 1818 – died February 20, 1895) was a formerly enslaved person who became one of the most powerful voices for abolition, human rights, and social justice in American history. Through his writing, speeches, and activism, Douglass fought tirelessly for the end of slavery, equal rights for African Americans, and justice for all marginalized groups. His autobiographies are some of the most important firsthand accounts of slavery and freedom in American history.

How Did Frederick Douglass Change the World?

Through his autobiographies and speeches, Frederick Douglass became a powerful voice for freedom and equality, advocating for the end of slavery, equal rights for African Americans, and women’s suffrage, while advising President Lincoln during the Civil War and working to ensure the passage of the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments, ultimately leaving a lasting legacy as a symbol of resistance, education, and justice for oppressed people worldwide.
